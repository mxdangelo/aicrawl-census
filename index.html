AI Crawler Observatory · Italy

In Italy, resistance to AI crawlers is concentrated in one sector: the press.

News blocks OpenAI’s GPTBot at 31% and media at 21%; no other sector exceeds 10%. 9.0% overall.

The observatory tracks three signals Italian sites send AI — whether they block crawlers, offer content, or reserve their rights.

543 domains · 10 sectors · 20 crawlers · snapshot 2 · 2026-07-14 · Method →

Block

Sites keep AI crawlers out through robots.txt — a plain-text file that can name a crawler and disallow paths. Each bar is one crawler; the split shows how the 543 sites treat it: blocked at the root, partial (some paths off limits), or allowed. Use the menus to slice by sector, or by whether a rule names the crawler or comes from a catch-all *.

Offer

The opposite move. llms.txt (a 2024 convention) is a Markdown file that offers a curated view of a site to language models — “here is what to read about us.” Almost nobody uses it: 46 of 543 sites, 8.5%. And the lead is an unexpected sector — banking, where more than 1 in 4 sites publish one, and every one is hand-written, not shipped by a plugin. Ink marks a deliberate file; gray, a plugin default.

Reserve

The legal one. Under EU copyright law (Directive 2019/790, Article 4), AI companies may text-and-data-mine public content for training unless the owner reserves the right in a machine-readable form. TDMRep is that reservation — the only one of the three signals with legal weight in the EU. Just 8 of 543 sites assert it, and they cluster in the industries that live on copyright: book publishers and universities (6), and the press (2). Every other sector: none.

Method

The sample. 543 Italian websites, stratified across 10 sectors, drawn from hand-curated sector lists cross-checked against the Tranco top-1M list filtered to .it. Latest snapshot: 2026-07-14. The observatory is built to re-run: each crawl is one snapshot, so the same measures can be tracked over time and, later, across countries.

The three signals. For every site we fetch and validate three files:

  • robots.txt, parsed under RFC 9309 (longest-match, Allow wins ties). A crawler’s verdict is blocked when the root path is denied, partial when only some paths are, allowed otherwise. A rule is specific when the site names the crawler, wildcard when it comes from a catch-all User-agent: *.
  • llms.txt, a Markdown file offering content to models. Hand-written vs plugin default is inferred from generator signatures and near-duplicate detection across sites.
  • tdmrep.json / TDMRep, the EU legal reservation, read through all three spec channels (a well-known file, HTTP headers, and HTML meta tags).

Presence is validated, never assumed. A 200 OK proves nothing — many servers answer 200 with an HTML page on any path. A file counts only if it is structurally valid; soft-404s are excluded.

Limits. robots.txt measures declared intent, not crawler behaviour. The CMS/plugin fingerprint is heuristic. “Not mentioned” is a default, not a decision, and is kept separate from explicit permission throughout.
